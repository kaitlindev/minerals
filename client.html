What are minerals?

Minerals are solid, inorganic and naturally occurring chemical compounds with definite internal structure. They are unable to exist as liquids or gases at standard room temperatures and pressures. Any substances created by a living organism (for example: amber and coral) do not classify as a mineral. All atoms within a mineral are arranged systematically, forming specific shapes and sizes. The Earth’s crust holds many different minerals with varying chemical compositions including silicon and oxygen. A few minerals have unique properties, such as being radioactive, magnetic or luminescent. Minerals should not be confused with rocks, which are either aggregates of several different minerals or contain no minerals without a distinct composition. Gemstones used for jewelry are cut and polished minerals.

Common elements found within minerals are oxygen (O), silicon (Si), aluminum (Al), iron (Fe), calcium (Ca), sodium (Na), potassium (K), and magnesium (Mg). Every mineral contains a distinctive arrangement of elements which determines its type and to which class it belongs to. The composition of these elements can determine its crystalline structure and physical properties. Oftentimes, different minerals can contain the same chemical composition, yet have contrasting physical traits. For example, both diamond and graphite are composed of pure carbon (C). Despite this, the carbon atoms within diamonds form a three dimensional lattice while the carbon atoms within graphite form a two dimensional network. Because of this, graphite is soft, dull and opaque while diamonds are transparent, rare, and form the hardest natural substance in the world.

How are they formed?

Minerals are formed from a variety of geological processes and determine its chemical composition and physical structure.

When magma is expelled from an active volcano, it becomes lava as it flows onto the surface of earth’s crust. The cooling and solidification of lava can develop igneous rocks through the process of crystallization. When specific conditions are met, igneous rocks can contain mineral deposits, which are called ores. These deposits vary in chemical composition and have the ability to produce differing classes of minerals. Most minerals formed through this process are crystalline.

Minerals can also be found in sedimentary rocks. These rocks are formed by the deposition and cementation of material which was collected through the process of weathering and erosion of Earth’s surface. This material is often transported from a source to a destination through the movement of wind, water or ice. In areas of low precipitation or high evaporation temperatures, seawater can evaporate, leaving behind deposits of minerals. Seawater can contain chemicals such as sodium chloride, calcium, sulfate and gypsum, which are essential elements found within minerals. The most common minerals found in sedimentary rocks are quartz and calcite.

Metamorphic rocks are formed when an existing type of rock (called the protolith) is transformed under substantial amounts of either heat or pressure. Any temperatures greater than 150-200 degree Celsius or pressure above 1500 bars (~100000 Pa) can cause significant change to the structure of the rock. Minerals formed through these processes are known as index minerals (which include garnet). Each index mineral is the result of the minimum pressure and/or temperature the protolith must have achieved for the formation of that mineral.

How are they classified?

Minerals are classified and organized by a variety of chemical properties that make them each unique. There are two groups of minerals: silicates and non-silicates. Each group contains multiple classes.

Silicates

Silicates are formed by the combination of two of the most abundant elements found in the crust of the Earth: oxygen (O) and silicon (Si). They form the largest class of minerals. Silicates have a silicon-oxygen tetrahedron chemical structure - one silicon ion for every four oxygen ions. The ability for silicates to link together their tetrahedrons is known as polymerization. There are 6 different groups of silicates.

Non-silicates

Non-silicate minerals are subdivided into groups based on their dominant chemical compositions. They constitute only 8% of the Earth’s crust. While most minerals are relatively rare, some are widespread across the globe. Physical Properties

Minerals are also categorized by their physical properties. Different chemical compositions lead to an assortment of qualities that are studied by geologists and mineralogists.

Mohs

The Mohs scale of mineral hardness is a qualitative ordinal scale that was created by German geologist and mineralogist Friedrich Mohs in 1812. The scale is used to determine the visible scratch resistance of a mineral when a hard mineral scratches the surface of a softer mineral. The scale begins at 1 (least hard minerals) and ends at 10 (most hard minerals).

Fracture

The fracture of a mineral is the texture of its surface when it is cracked or split. The cleavage of a mineral is the way a mineral breaks along clean and designated directions. The fracture can be seen on all minerals but will happen in different ways. Fractures can be described as being smooth, irregular, splintery or jagged.

Specific Gravity

The specific gravity (SG) of a mineral is the measure of its density. The weight of a mineral sample is compared against its relative weight in water. Water has a specific gravity of 1.0. Mineral that have a SG of less than 2 are considered light, while those with a SG between 2 and 4.5 is average and greater than 4.5 is heavy.

PHP's MySQLi

MySQLi (MySQL Improved) Extension is a relational database driver used to provide an interface with MySQL that was introduced with PHP version 5.0.0. MySQLi is an improvement on the older version of the PHP MySQL driver with sophisticated benefits, such as an object-oriented interface, debugging support and support for multiple statements. The MySQLi functions are used to access database servers found with MySQL version 4.1.13 or newer and can be accessed through multiple mysqli classes, such as mysqli, mysqli_stmt, mysqli_result, mysqli_driver, mysqli_warning, and mysqli_sql_exception. For this app, we are only using two classes: mysqli and mysqli_result.

mysqli class

The methods used within the mysqli class are:

  • mysqli_connect() - Establishes a connection to a MySQL database by using the required credentials.
  • mysqli_close() - Closes an opened database connection.
  • mysqli_connect_errno() - If there is an error connecting to the database, return the error code.
  • mysqli_connect_error() - If there is an error connecting to the database, return the error description.
  • mysqli_query() - Runs a query on the database.
mysqli_result class

The methods used within the mysqli_result class are:

  • mysqli_fetch_array() - The results of a query on a database are returned one per row as an array.

Minerals Database

The MySQL database that is being used for this app is small (only 92 rows) and contains one table with ten columns. The table is called “minerals” and the structure is as follows:

  • id - primary key; auto-increment; int(2)
  • name - varchar(30)
  • composition - varchar(40)
  • group - varchar(30)
  • class - varchar(30)
  • color - varchar(30)
  • mohs - decimal(3,1)
  • fracture - varchar(30)
  • cleavage - varchar(30)
  • tenacity - varchar(30)

AJAX + JQuery

This app queries the minerals database based on seven different criteria: name, the letter that it starts with, composition, class, color, fracture and mohs value. For each of these criteria, there is a function that is triggered by a user event (in this case, a button click or the movement of a JQuery UI Slider). These functions contain ajax calls that both send and retrieve data from a PHP script. Each button is assigned a unique value that can be used to perform a query on the database. For example, there is a button with a value of "blue" that is used to query the database for all minerals that are colored blue. When this button is clicked, the value of "blue" is retrieved using a JQuery function and sent through a function search_color() (explained below). That function "POSTS" the string "blue" as data through an ajax call to the url "server.php" which contains the PHP code that connects to the MySQL database. It is saved as a PHP variable where it is then fed into a query that can return an array of all matching results. These results are echoed, and on the successful completion of the function, are then posted to a div on the page, #content, using html().

Ajax/JQuery

By importing the JQuery library, you can perform an asynchronous http request with $.ajax. The following function is needed to form the connection between client and server:

$(document).ready(function() { 
	$("#selectAllBtn").click(function() {
		 function show_all() { 
			$.ajax({ 
				type: "POST", 
				url: "server.php", 
				data: {action: "showAll"}, 
				success: function(data) { 
					$("#content").html(data); 
				}
			  });
		 } 
		show_all(); 
	}); 
});

In the above function, clicking the button #selectAllBtn triggers a function on document ready that calls $.ajax. The data is simply a key-value pair: the string "showAll" is assigned to "action" as a way to describe what data is being sent. The string "showAll" will be stored in a PHP variable in server.php. If the ajax call is successful, all rows that are stored in the minerals database will be returned and be appended to the <div id="content"></div> which can be seen on client.html almost instantaneously.

It is necessary to determine which search method is being used by the user at any given moment based on click events, so the if statement above is used to determine that the "Search All" button has been clicked. First, we check that $action holds "show all" and if so, we can write a query and store it in $query. Now, we can query the database using the mysqli_query() function, only if there is no error. Before we print out the results of the query, we must build a table element to organize the data and display it correctly. The final results should mimic the actual structure of the existing minerals database and look very similar. To start building the table, we echo the table header that we created earlier. Now, we are ready to echo the results of the query.

Using mysqli_fetch_array(), each hit from the query will be stored in its own row. These rows are stored in an array that can be traversed and printed out while following the structure that was set forth by the table header. As long as there exists another row of the query results to access, it will be echoed out and appended to the #content div. Whenever a search is initialized , the table will be replaced with the results of the new query. After the query is finished, the connection to the database can be closed using mysqli_close().

Once again, we are using the $.ajax function within JQuery to interact with the PHP code. The value entered into the input field needs to be sent to the PHP file so that it can be used within the query. The search_name() function that then fires an ajax call is similar to show_all(), except that the value entered into the input field must be stored into a variable before it is used within the AJAX call. 	$("#nameSearchBtn").click(function() {
		 function search_name() { 
			var name=$("#nameSearchInput").val();
			$.ajax({ 
				type: "POST",

if ($mineral_name != null) { 
	$query = "SELECT * FROM minerals WHERE name LIKE '%$mineral_name%'";

The value of $mineral_name will remain null unless there exist characters within the input field AND the user also clicked the "Search" button. Even if the user does not enter any text into the input field and clicks "Search", the value will still remain null. Only when the value becomes non-null will the query be used. The query is stored in $query and $show holds the result of that query using the database connection that was opened, $con. Once more, the table header is echoed before the actual results are displayed in order to form a table.

This structure of query (involving LIKE and containing a wild card character) is also how the mineral database is searched for by letter and chemical composition.

The query used to search by the letter that it starts with uses a different kind of wild card where the % is only at the end of the keyword: 

$query = "SELECT * FROM minerals WHERE name LIKE '$mineral_letter%'";

The query for chemical composition allows the user to search for only one element at a time. Because minerals can contain many different elements to form a chemical compound, the pattern wildcard is used again like in the name search. The selected element (by clicking on the corresponding button) can be found anywhere in the composition string stored within the database, so a % is needed both before and after the element value: 

$query = "SELECT * FROM minerals WHERE composition LIKE '%$mineral_composition%'";

Using mysqli_fetch_array(), each hit from the query will be stored in its own row that will be echoed out into the table we created. Ajax/JQuery

Function getStarsByConstellation() collects the value (constellation name) of the option that is selected from the dropdown list. When the user selects his/her first option or selects a new option, the onchange Javascript event is triggered.

function getStarsByConstellation() { 
	$.ajax({
		url: "/server.php", // receiving URL
		type: "POST", // POST request
		data: "star_constellation=" + $('#star_constellation').val(), // data is formed by the value of the id 'star_name'
		dataType: "json", // data is sent/received in JSON format
		success: function (response, status, http) { // if successful... 
			console.log(response);
			 var results = ''; // form a string to append to the <p> with id 'star_results' 
			$.each (response, function(index, item) { // for each item, find the name, distance and constellation and add to results
				results = results + "<div>Name: " + item.name + "<br/>";
				results = results + "Distance: " + item.distance + "<br/>"; 
				results = results + "Constellaton: " + item.constellation + "<br/>";
				results = results + "</div>";
			});
			$('#star_results').html(results); // append with results to the <p>
		}, 
		error: function (http, status, error) { // if there's an error, show user
			alert("Error: " + error);
		}
	});
}

Results are stored in the $results array after the $constellation value is matched to each star object's constellation attrbute ['constellation']. First, we need to check that $constellation is not null, and if it's not, we use a for loop to find matches: 

if ($constellation != null) { 
	for( $count = 0 ; $count < count( $star_data ) ; $count++ ) { 
		if( stripos( $star_data[$count]['constellation'] , $constellation ) !== false ) { 
			array_push( $results , $star_data[$count] ); 
		} 
	} 
}
